Call for Data

Help Build LabOS for Scientific Vision and Reasoning

LabOS is collecting high-quality multimodal wet-lab data to train and evaluate scientific vision-language models grounded in real laboratory workflows, protocol execution, visual manipulation, and error understanding.

Scientific AI Wet-Lab Understanding Multimodal Data Contributions

Ideal contributions

  • Authentic wet-lab procedures rather than staged demos
  • Protocol-aligned image or video data
  • Annotations, metadata, and error cases when available

Submission channels

www.ai4labos.com

congle@stanford.edu mengdiw@princeton.edu
Priority is given to data that reflects real experimental workflows, real-world variability, and practical laboratory reasoning.

What We Are Looking For

First-person or third-person laboratory videos
Image or video data aligned with written experimental protocols
Step-by-step annotations of laboratory procedures
Error, issue, or deviation annotations during execution
Multi-view recordings of the same procedure
Metadata such as protocol name, scene type, point of view, or task category
Optional audio-aligned or outcome-aligned recordings
Best-fit datasets show what scientists actually do, what they see, and how real procedures unfold in practice.

Preferred Recording Setup

Preferred scene quality

  • Clear visibility of both the operator and the object being manipulated
  • Realistic wet-lab tasks involving hand-object interaction
  • Natural laboratory environments with clutter, occlusion, and real-world variability
  • Varied subject positions and scales within the frame

Preferred diversity

  • Diverse camera angles and viewpoints
  • Multiple perspectives of the same procedure when available
  • Protocol-aligned recordings with step-level correspondence
  • Both successful runs and runs containing common mistakes or deviations

Accepted capture devices

Smart glasses Action cameras Smartphones Webcams Fixed lab cameras

A single camera is acceptable, but multi-view recording is preferred whenever possible.

Useful viewpoints

  • Egocentric or head-mounted view
  • Bench-side left or right view
  • Rear view behind the operator
  • Chest-mounted close-manipulation view
  • Top-down or overhead view
  • Robotic wrist or system-mounted view
Minimum framing rule: at least one camera should maintain a clear view of both the human subject and the object or sample throughout the procedure.

Quality and Annotation Requirements

Data quality expectations

  • Stable recording with sufficient resolution
  • Clear visual access to key actions, tools, and materials
  • Minimal heavy filtering or post-processing
  • Consistent framing during important steps
  • Accurate protocol alignment
  • Documentation of missing, obscured, or failed steps

Models should be able to distinguish

  • Visible actions
  • Contextual waiting or setup states
  • Errors or deviations
  • Steps that occurred but were not visible

Useful annotations and metadata

  • Protocol title and step-level timestamps
  • Action labels and context or state labels
  • Error annotations and notes for non-visible steps
  • Point-of-view information and device type
  • Recording date and site or lab identifier
  • Outcome or result references when appropriate

Preferred formats

Structured .txt or .json

Annotated data is highly valuable and directly increases usability for model training and evaluation.

High-value submissions do not just show the procedure. They make the procedure legible.

Submission Package, Permissions, and Impact

What to include

  • Raw video or image files
  • Reference protocol or task document
  • Aligned annotations and error labels if available
  • Basic metadata and file descriptions
  • Capture device and camera-position information
  • Multi-view correspondence for the same session
  • Permission and usage-rights confirmation

Rights, permissions, and safety

  • Submit only data you are authorized to share
  • Data may be used for research and model development
  • Submissions must not violate privacy, confidentiality, or institutional policy
  • Required permissions or consents must already be obtained
  • Do not include sensitive personal information or restricted materials

Why contribute

  • Improve wet-lab procedure understanding
  • Align visual activity with written protocols
  • Detect mistakes and deviations
  • Improve reproducibility
  • Support real-time scientific assistance

Progress in laboratory AI depends on high-quality, real-world multimodal data contributed by the scientific community.

A complete submission is one that is useful, shareable, and interpretable without follow-up guesswork.

How to Submit

Submission process

  1. Prepare your files and folders.
  2. Write a short description of the procedure or dataset.
  3. Add protocol and annotation files when available.
  4. Include metadata for device, viewpoint, and task type.
  5. Note known errors, deviations, or visibility limitations.
